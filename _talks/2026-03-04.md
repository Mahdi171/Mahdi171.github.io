<ul>
<li>Quantum computers pose a serious threat to blockchains that rely on classical signatures such as <strong>ECDSA</strong>, since exposed public keys could allow adversaries to recover private keys using quantum algorithms.</li>

<li>Existing proposals for migrating to post-quantum signatures often require <strong>changing addresses</strong>, deploying hybrid signature schemes, or introducing mechanisms that fail to protect <strong>inactive accounts</strong>.</li>

<li>We observe that blockchains using <strong>EdDSA</strong> signatures (such as Sui, Solana, and Near) have a structural advantage: their keys are deterministically derived from a <strong>seed</strong> following <strong>RFC 8032</strong>.</li>

<li>This property enables a user to prove ownership of an account by proving knowledge of the <strong>EdDSA seed</strong> in <strong>zero knowledge</strong>, without revealing elliptic-curve secrets.</li>

<li>We introduce a protocol where the EdDSA seed is used as a witness in a zero-knowledge proof that authorizes a new <strong>post-quantum signature key</strong>.</li>

<li>The protocol enables <strong>seamless migration to quantum-safe signatures</strong> while preserving existing addresses and maintaining backward compatibility.</li>

<li>Importantly, the approach also works when the <strong>public key has already been exposed</strong>, which is the common case for active blockchain accounts.</li>

<li>We formalize a <strong>security model for post-quantum ready signatures</strong> and prove the security of the construction through a <strong>game-based argument</strong>.</li>

<li>A proof-of-concept implementation demonstrates practicality, achieving about <strong>6.2 seconds proving time, 2.3 seconds verification time, and a 5.4 MB proof</strong> using the <strong>Ligetron zkVM</strong>.</li>
</ul>

Cryptography sometimes hides clever time capsules. EdDSA’s deterministic seed derivation was designed for simplicity and safety, yet it quietly leaves a door open for zero-knowledge based upgrades. Years later, that door turns out to be a surprisingly elegant escape hatch from the quantum future.
